Homeless population keeps growing despite City efforts

This will be a summary of my story.

By Evelina Nedlund

Evelina Nedlund

Homelessness in New York City has been on the rise over the past five years. Even though numerous attempts to address the issue have been made by authorities, nothing seems to have had a long-lasting effect on reducing the amount of people staying in shelters.

“For me, the more important question is what the city is not doing,” said Deborah Padgett, a New York University professor and researcher on homelessness. “It is not diverting funds and resources from the shelter system to pay for rental vouchers and supportive housing.”

The national policy pushed by the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) is “housing first”, but Padgett said the city is defying this and hiding behind the legal “right to shelter” to defend its heavy emphasis on shelters.

“There is a ‘homeless services industry’ that has grown up since the 1990’s that depends on billions of public funds to keep it going,” she said. “The status quo satisfies more powerful interests than changing toward housing first and ending homelessness.”

“NYC is a ‘perfect storm’ of barriers including rising rents, lower wages and shrinking stock of affordable apartments”

Explaining why homelessness in New York has been increasing for years on end is not an easy task, but according to Padgett it is caused by a mixture of several factors.

“It is difficult to say why so much in NYC when most of the nation has seen decreases. But NYC is a ‘perfect storm’ of barriers including rising rents, lower wages and shrinking stock of affordable apartments,” Padgett said.
