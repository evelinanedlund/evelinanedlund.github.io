Three thousand people march for climate action in NYC

The Rise for Climate, Jobs and Justice march gathered people carrying creative banners, signs and costumes in Battery Park on Thursday.

By Evelina Nedlund

The sweltering heat with temperatures soaring into the 90s did not stop an estimated 3,000 people from marching through Lower Manhattan on Thursday to protest politician’s unwillingness to tackle environmental issues – including global warming.

"There are a lot of people in New York who support the climate justice movement."

This weekend people are joining more than 800 actions in 91 countries under the banner of Rise for Climate to demonstrate the urgency of the climate crisis. Actions in the US and around the world are happening on September 8, but in order to not conflict with the NYC Labor Day Parade organizers chose to hold the event in New York City on Thursday instead of during the weekend.

The events are being coordinated by a group called 350.org which is an international environmental organization, but for every event there are several local organizers and volunteers involved as well.

”I think it is very efficient that we are doing this because in New York there are a lot of people who support the climate justice movement,” said Maham Rasheda, 16, who is a member of the non-profit organization Global Kids and was one of the speakers at the march.

The Rise for Climate, Jobs and Justice march gathered people carrying creative banners, signs and costumes in Battery Park. Signs carrying messages such as “Make corporations pay for their damage” and “We rise against fossil fuels” were mixed with people dressed in costumes ranging from Mother Earth to jellyfishes. Their mutual aim was to urge politicians to immediately act on issues related to the environment and climate change.

“It came to me that if we do not have clean air and clean water none of the other things I was fighting for are going to matter,” said Helen Sacks Heisler, 69, who attended the march and lives on the Upper West Side and have marched for many different causes over the past 40-something years. “So, we need to fix this first in order to focus on other issues.”

Sacks Heisler is a grandchild of the holocaust as her parents came from Germany, and she said that she has been aware of injustice her whole life.

“Political parties or stances should not matter in regard to this issue.”

“As issues come up I combat all of them,” she said. “Political parties or stances should not matter in regard to this issue.”

Participants aimed to demand local leaders in NYC to commit to stop fossil fuel, shift to renewable energy and penalize corporate polluters.

“We are here today to demand real solutions, particularly from our New York governor (Andrew) Cuomo and mayor (Bill) de Blasio,” said Lee Ziesche who is an organizer with Sane Energy and Seeding Sovereignty, which are two of the participating groups and organizers of the march.
